How to Read a Certificate of Analysis
(Without Getting Fooled)

A "99% pure" COA means nothing if the vendor doesn't show the other 4 panels. Here's exactly what to look for — and what they're hoping you won't notice.

The 5 Things a Real COA Should Show

1. HPLC Purity (%)

What it tells you: The percentage of the main peptide peak vs. all other peptide-related peaks.

The trap: A 99% pure peptide can still be 40% residual solvent by weight. HPLC only measures peptide-related impurities — NOT solvents, metals, or TFA. This is the #1 thing vendors exploit. They show you 99.X% and you think you're done.

Green flag: Purity ≥98.5% with a visible 3rd party lab name.

Red flag: "99.X%" with no other panels and no lab name.

2. Quantitative TFA Content (% or ppm)

What it tells you: How much trifluoroacetic acid counter-ion is in your peptide.

Why it matters: Peptides are often sold as TFA salts. Without quantitative TFA, your "10mg" vial could contain 7mg peptide + 3mg TFA. You're paying for acid.

Green flag: TFA ≤1% or ≤10,000 ppm. Quantified, not just "TFA removed."

Red flag: "TFA removed" claim with no numbers. Or TFA completely absent from the COA.

3. Residual Solvents (GC-MS or HS-GC)

What it tells you: Whether manufacturing solvents (acetonitrile, DMF, dichloromethane) are still present.

Why it matters: A 2023 Belgian study found 5 of 12 "high purity" peptide vendors had residual solvent levels above safety thresholds. This is the invisible risk. Finnrick includes solvent screens in their standard panel — but most vendor COAs skip it entirely.

Green flag: Solvent panel visible with all values below USP/ICH limits.

Red flag: No solvent data at all. Or "residual solvents: compliant" with no actual numbers.

4. Heavy Metals (ICP-MS)

What it tells you: Whether toxic catalyst metals (palladium, platinum, etc.) from synthesis remain.

Why it matters: Palladium is a common peptide synthesis catalyst with strict limits for pharmaceuticals (typically ≤10 ppm). Almost no peptide vendors test for it voluntarily. If a vendor shows ICP-MS data, that's a massive trust signal.

Green flag: ICP-MS panel with Pd/Pt/other metals below pharma limits.

Red flag: No metals testing. This is the norm — but the best vendors do it anyway.

5. Endotoxin (LAL Test)

What it tells you: Bacterial endotoxin contamination levels (EU/mg).

Why it matters: Endotoxins cause immune reactions, fever, and inflammation. Critical for anything injected. Finnrick now offers this as a paid add-on — very few vendors include it by default. If a vendor voluntarily shows negative endotoxin results, that's a huge trust signal.

Green flag: Endotoxin <0.5 EU/mg, voluntarily included.

Red flag: No endotoxin data (common but worth noting).

Red Flags That Scream "Don't Trust This COA"

❌ No batch/lot number

If you can't match the COA to a specific batch, it's worthless.

❌ No test date

A COA from 2023 doesn't tell you anything about what you're buying in 2026.

❌ "99.X%" with no other panels

The classic move. HPLC only, no contamination data. Assume 30%+ of that vial is stuff you don't want.

❌ Self-issued COA, no 3rd party lab

If the vendor wrote their own COA with no independent lab name, it's marketing, not science.

❌ PDF that looks like WordArt

Real lab COAs look clinical. If it looks like someone made it in Microsoft Word, it probably was.

❌ "TFA removed" — no numbers

TFA removal is a claim. Quantitative TFA is a measurement. Always ask for the number.

Green Flags — Signs of a Vendor You Can Actually Trust

✅ 3rd party lab name visible

Janoshik, MZ Biolabs, Finnrick, Chromate, BTLabs — real labs with public reputations. The name should be on the COA.

✅ Batch number matches your vial

The best vendors let you type your lot number into a verification page.

✅ Full panel: HPLC + TFA + Solvents + Metals

Rare, but gold. Paradigm Peptide and Peptide Partners are the standouts here.

✅ Endotoxin included voluntarily

Almost nobody does this. If a vendor pays for endotoxin testing and publishes results, they're serious about quality.

✅ Finnrick-tested with public results

A vendor who submits to Finnrick (or whose customers do) and the results are public — that's the gold standard for independent verification.

✅ Multi-lab 2026 batches

Vendor shows recent (2026) COAs from 2+ independent labs. Finnrick + Janoshik, or Finnrick + MZ Biolabs. Eliminates single-lab bias.
