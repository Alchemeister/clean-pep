Verification guide

Read the COA before you read the marketing.

A purity percentage by itself is not a full quality story. Use this checklist to separate useful third-party evidence from decorative PDFs.

1. Identity and batch
Look for peptide identity, lot or batch number, test date, and a lab report identifier you can verify.
2. HPLC purity
Useful baseline, but it does not cover every contamination risk by itself.
3. Quantity
Check whether the vial contains the claimed amount, not just whether the main peak is clean.
4. Extra panels
Residual solvents, TFA, heavy metals, and endotoxin testing are stronger transparency signals when available.
5. Independent lookup
Open the lab or Finnrick verification source directly. Do not rely on a screenshot alone.
6. Match the physical vial
If batch, date, vendor, or visible vial details do not match, treat the COA as unproven for that item.
Red flag: A PDF that says 99% purity but has no independent lab lookup, batch number, date, or quantity result is not enough evidence.
